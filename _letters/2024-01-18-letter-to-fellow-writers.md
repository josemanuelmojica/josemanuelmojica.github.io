Dear fellow writer,

You know who you are. You're the one staring at the blank page, cursor blinking with what feels like accusation. You're the one wondering if anyone will read what you write, if your thoughts are worth sharing, if you have anything original to contribute to the vast sea of existing words.

I'm writing this letter because I know these feelings intimately. They visit me regularly, usually at 2 AM when the writing isn't flowing and the inner critic is loudest.

## On Permission

First, let me say something you might need to hear: **You don't need permission to write.** Not from editors, not from other writers, not from the imaginary audience in your head passing judgment on every sentence.

The act of writing is its own justification. Every time you put words to page—whether it's a journal entry, a text message, or a carefully crafted essay—you're participating in one of humanity's most fundamental activities: making meaning through language.

## On Originality

You worry about originality, about having something new to say. But here's what I've learned: originality isn't about inventing entirely new ideas. It's about bringing your particular perspective, your unique combination of experiences and observations, to the universal themes we all wrestle with.

Your voice—the one that emerges when you stop trying to sound like someone else—is the only truly original thing you have to offer. And it's enough. More than enough.

## On Audience

Maybe you're writing for an audience of one (yourself), or maybe you're hoping to reach thousands. Both are valid. But remember that every great writer started with an audience of one. They wrote first for themselves, to understand their own thoughts, to capture their own experience.

If your writing resonates with you—if it helps you think more clearly, feel more deeply, or connect more authentically with your own experience—then it's already succeeded. Everything else is bonus.

## On Imperfection

Your first draft will be imperfect. So will your second. And your tenth. This isn't a bug in the writing process; it's a feature. Writing isn't about achieving perfection on the first try—it's about gradually discovering what you're trying to say through the act of saying it.

Give yourself permission to write badly. Give yourself permission to explore ideas you're not sure about, to ask questions you don't have answers to, to change your mind as you write.

## On Persistence

The difference between writers and people who want to write isn't talent or education or perfect circumstances. It's the willingness to keep writing even when it's difficult, even when the words don't come easily, even when you're not sure anyone is listening.

Write anyway. Write especially when you don't feel like it, when the inner critic is loud, when you're convinced you have nothing interesting to say. Those are often the times when your most honest, most necessary words appear.

## A Final Thought

The world needs your voice. Not because you'll solve all its problems or revolutionize literature, but because you see things in a way no one else does. Your particular combination of experiences, insights, and questions contributes something to the ongoing human conversation that no one else can contribute.

So write. Write badly if you must, but write. Write for yourself first, then for whoever might find value in your words. Write because the act of writing is itself worthwhile, regardless of what comes after.

The blank page isn't your enemy. It's your invitation.

With solidarity and encouragement,  
A fellow traveler on the writing path

---

*P.S. If these words find you in a moment of doubt, know that you're not alone. Every writer faces these struggles. The act of writing through them is part of what makes us writers.*
